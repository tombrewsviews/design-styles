# Classic Design Movements

## 1. Swiss Style / International Typographic Style (1950s-Present)

**Core principle**: Clarity, objectivity, and systematic design through mathematical grids and typography. Communication should be universal, unbiased, and transcend cultural boundaries through rational visual organization.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Strict grid systems with mathematical proportions governing all element placement
- Asymmetric layouts with clear visual hierarchy based on information importance
- Photography preferred over illustration for objective, documentary imagery
- Flush-left, ragged-right text alignment for natural reading flow
- Generous use of white space as an active design element
- Information organized by visual weight and systematic spacing
- Clean, uncluttered compositions with purposeful element placement
- Sans-serif typography as default for modern, neutral communication

**Color palette**: Often limited to black, white, and one or two bold accent colors. Red is common as a primary accent. When color is used, it's purposeful and bold rather than decorative. High contrast between elements.

**Typography**: Grotesque and neo-grotesque sans-serifs dominate—Helvetica, Akzidenz-Grotesk, Univers, Neue Haas Grotesk. Mathematical type scaling (often based on ratios). Clear functional hierarchy achieved through size, weight, and position. Single typeface family typically used throughout.

**Key elements**: Visible or implied grid structures, documentary photography, generous negative space, thin ruled lines as dividers, systematic spacing units, objective information presentation, mathematical proportions.

**Feeling**: Objective, universal, clear, authoritative, timeless, professional, democratic, rational, trustworthy, neutral.

**Key figures**: Josef Müller-Brockmann, Armin Hofmann, Emil Ruder, Max Bill, Karl Gerstner, Ernst Keller, Theo Ballmer.

**Era context**: Post-WWII rationalism in Switzerland, desire for universal visual communication transcending language barriers, reaction against wartime propaganda, influence of Bauhaus refugees, Swiss neutrality reflected in design philosophy.

**Prompt keywords**: `swiss style design, international typographic style, helvetica grid, müller-brockmann poster, grid composition, objective typography, clean minimal poster, asymmetric layout, swiss graphic design`

## 2. Bauhaus (1919-1933, Ongoing Influence)

**Core principle**: "Form follows function." Unity of art, craft, and technology. Geometric reduction to essential forms. Design should be accessible, democratic, and serve society through mass production. Art and industry united.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Primary geometric shapes as building blocks (circle, square, triangle)
- Primary colors (red, yellow, blue) plus black and white as foundational palette
- Grid-based, modular compositions allowing for systematic design
- Asymmetric balance creating dynamic tension
- Complete absence of ornamentation or decorative elements
- Industrial materials aesthetic celebrating machine production
- Strong diagonal, horizontal, and vertical compositions
- Photomontage combining photography with graphic elements
- Integration of text and image in unified compositions

**Color palette**: Primary red, yellow, blue used boldly and flatly. Black, white, gray as structural colors. Occasionally earth tones from the earlier Weimar period. Colors are unmodulated, solid, and symbolic rather than naturalistic.

**Typography**: Geometric sans-serifs reign—Futura, Universal (Herbert Bayer), Bauhaus typeface. Strong preference for lowercase ("why use two alphabets when one will do"). Functional clarity over decorative beauty. Experimental layouts with type at angles, in circles, or following geometric paths.

**Key elements**: Geometric abstraction, overlapping primary shapes, modular grid systems, functional everyday objects elevated as design, photomontage techniques, primary color combinations, clean angular forms, circles and triangles as motifs.

**Feeling**: Rational, ordered, universal, functional, modern, democratic, utopian, progressive, optimistic, egalitarian.

**Key figures**: Walter Gropius (founder), László Moholy-Nagy, Wassily Kandinsky, Paul Klee, Josef Albers, Herbert Bayer, Oskar Schlemmer, Marcel Breuer, Ludwig Mies van der Rohe.

**Era context**: German Weimar Republic (1919-1933), post-WWI reconstruction, industrial revolution's maturation, socialist ideals of design for all, art education reform merging fine art with practical crafts, Nazi closure in 1933 spreading influence globally.

**Prompt keywords**: `bauhaus design, geometric primary colors, form follows function, modernist design, primary shapes circle square triangle, herbert bayer typography, kandinsky geometric, weimar bauhaus, bauhaus poster`

## 3. Constructivism (1913-1930s)

**Core principle**: Art must serve revolutionary social purposes. Geometric abstraction as visual propaganda for building a new society. Art as construction, not mystical creation. Artists are engineers and builders of the new world.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Bold diagonal compositions creating dynamic tension and forward movement
- Geometric shapes positioned at sharp, energetic angles
- Photomontage techniques combining photography with graphic elements
- Severely limited color palette with red, black, white dominant
- Strong visual hierarchy directing the eye with purpose
- Industrial and mechanical imagery celebrating workers and machines
- Text integrated as graphic element, not separate from image
- Dramatic scale contrasts between elements
- Asymmetric compositions suggesting motion and progress

**Color palette**: Revolutionary red (symbolizing workers' blood and communist ideals), black (print/industry), white (purity/new beginning). Occasionally metallic gold or silver. High contrast combinations creating visual impact for mass communication.

**Typography**: Bold geometric sans-serifs with maximum impact. Diagonal and angled placement creating energy. Dramatic scale contrasts between headlines and body text. Cyrillic letterforms featured prominently. Type treated as image. Stencil-like industrial forms.

**Key elements**: Strong diagonal lines suggesting movement forward, photomontage of workers/machines/crowds, industrial imagery (gears, factories, buildings), geometric shapes in dynamic arrangements, revolutionary symbols (red stars, hammers, sickles), crowds and masses, typography as propaganda.

**Feeling**: Dynamic, revolutionary, powerful, urgent, propagandistic, industrial, energetic, collective, optimistic about social transformation, confrontational.

**Key figures**: Alexander Rodchenko, El Lissitzky, Varvara Stepanova, Lyubov Popova, Gustav Klutsis, Vladimir Tatlin, Alexei Gan.

**Era context**: Russian Revolution (1917) and its aftermath, Soviet avant-garde art movement, rejection of "art for art's sake" in favor of socially useful art, utopian vision of new society, eventually suppressed by Socialist Realism under Stalin.

**Prompt keywords**: `constructivist design, russian constructivism, rodchenko poster, el lissitzky, red black propaganda poster, diagonal composition, revolutionary graphics, soviet avant-garde, beat the whites with the red wedge`

## 4. Art Deco (1920s-1940s)

**Core principle**: Glamour, luxury, and modernity expressed through geometric ornamentation and streamlined forms. Celebration of progress, speed, and machine age elegance. Decorative modernism for the Jazz Age elite.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Geometric patterns repeated and layered (chevrons, sunbursts, zigzags, fan shapes)
- Symmetry and repetition creating rhythm and opulence
- Streamlined, aerodynamic forms suggesting speed and progress
- Luxurious material representations (gold, chrome, lacquer, marble, exotic woods)
- Stepped and tiered forms echoing skyscraper architecture
- Radiating lines suggesting energy and light
- Influences from Egyptian, Aztec, African, and Asian art filtered through modernism
- Bold outlines and high stylization of natural forms
- Vertical emphasis and elongated proportions

**Color palette**: Gold and black as signature combination. Silver, chrome, and cream. Rich jewel tones—emerald green, sapphire blue, ruby red, amethyst purple. High contrast pairings. Metallic accents throughout suggesting luxury.

**Typography**: Geometric display faces with strong character. Inline and outline letter styles. Broadway-style theatrical lettering. Bold serifs with sharp, chiseled details. Decorative capitals with geometric embellishments. Stylized sans-serifs with unique personality.

**Key elements**: Sunburst and fan motifs (often radiating from center), stylized flora and fauna (gazelles, panthers, eagles), skyscraper silhouettes, geometric borders and frames, chrome and glass details, ziggurat and stepped pyramid shapes, stylized female figures, exotic animals, fountains.

**Feeling**: Glamorous, luxurious, modern, optimistic, celebratory, sophisticated, aspirational, decadent, cosmopolitan, theatrical, confident.

**Era context**: Jazz Age and Roaring Twenties prosperity, post-WWI celebration and excess, machine age optimism, Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes (Paris, 1925), Hollywood glamour influence, Great Gatsby era.

**Prompt keywords**: `art deco design, 1920s glamour, geometric luxury, gold black design, sunburst pattern, gatsby aesthetic, chrysler building style, jazz age elegance, art deco poster, roaring twenties`

## 5. Art Nouveau (1890-1910)

**Core principle**: Organic forms and natural curves as a rejection of industrial standardization. "Total art" (Gesamtkunstwerk) unifying all design disciplines from architecture to jewelry. Art should be integrated into everyday life, not separated in museums.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Flowing, sinuous lines called "whiplash curves" inspired by natural growth
- Plant-based ornamentation pervading all elements (vines, flowers, leaves, stems, roots)
- Asymmetric compositions following organic, not geometric, logic
- Complete integration of structure and decoration—ornament is not applied but integral
- Feminine figures with impossibly flowing hair as central motifs
- Naturalistic imagery stylized into decorative patterns
- Ornamental borders and frames growing organically from content
- Vertical elongation and upward growth suggesting life force
- Stained glass effects with outlined color areas

**Color palette**: Muted, sophisticated naturals—olive green, sage, terra cotta, aged gold, deep purple, cream, dusty rose, peacock blue. Jewel-like accents for emphasis. Earthy but rich, like an autumnal garden.

**Typography**: Highly decorative custom letterforms with organic curves. Flowing serifs that seem to grow like vines. Letters integrated with surrounding imagery rather than separate. Hand-drawn custom alphabets for each project. Sinuous strokes and tendrils extending from letters.

**Key elements**: Vines and tendrils weaving through compositions, flowers (lilies, irises, roses, orchids), peacock feathers with their eye patterns, women with impossibly flowing hair, insects (dragonflies, butterflies, beetles), stained glass window effects, ornamental borders growing from corners, trees with decorative canopies.

**Feeling**: Organic, romantic, decorative, naturalistic, elegant, mystical, sensual, handcrafted, flowing, feminine, escapist.

**Key figures**: Alphonse Mucha (posters), Hector Guimard (Paris Metro), Gustav Klimt (painting), Louis Comfort Tiffany (glass), Aubrey Beardsley (illustration), Antoni Gaudí (architecture), René Lalique (jewelry), Émile Gallé (glass).

**Era context**: Reaction against industrial mass production's ugliness, influence of Japanese art (Japonisme) and its organic approach, Symbolism movement in literature and art, fin de siècle (end of century) culture, Arts and Crafts movement's values.

**Prompt keywords**: `art nouveau style, alphonse mucha poster, organic curves, flowing lines, botanical ornament, whiplash curve, decorative floral, stained glass aesthetic, mucha woman, art nouveau border`

## 6. Modernism (1900s-1970s)

**Core principle**: Rejection of ornament and historical tradition in favor of functionalism, rationality, and universal design solutions. "Less is more." Truth to materials. Form follows function. Design for the industrial age.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Clean geometric forms reduced to essentials
- Complete rejection of decoration and ornament ("ornament is crime" - Loos)
- Emphasis on function determining form, not tradition or style
- Honest expression of materials—steel looks like steel, concrete like concrete
- Open space and abundant light as design elements
- Asymmetric but carefully balanced compositions
- Industrial materials celebrated (steel, glass, concrete, plywood)
- Neutral, universal aesthetic transcending cultural specificity
- Modular, repeatable systems

**Color palette**: Neutral whites, grays, blacks dominating. Primary colors used sparingly and purposefully as accents. Natural material tones (wood grain, concrete gray, metal silver). Color is functional, not decorative.

**Typography**: Sans-serif fonts as default modern choice—Futura, Helvetica, Univers, Gill Sans. Clean letterforms stripped of historical references. Functional clarity prioritized over personality. Hierarchy achieved through size and weight only, not decoration.

**Key elements**: Geometric abstraction (circles, squares, rectangles), underlying grid systems, generous white space, industrial material aesthetic, honest structural expression, minimal ornamentation, functional forms serving purpose, modular components.

**Feeling**: Progressive, rational, universal, clean, forward-looking, democratic, honest, efficient, timeless, optimistic about human progress.

**Key figures**: Le Corbusier (architecture), Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, Walter Gropius, László Moholy-Nagy, Jan Tschichold (typography), Massimo Vignelli, Dieter Rams (product design).

**Sub-movements**: Bauhaus, De Stijl, International Style, Swiss Style, Functionalism, each with distinct character but shared core values.

**Era context**: Industrial revolution's maturation, two World Wars reshaping society, machine age demanding new forms, belief in progress through rational design, social housing movements, mass production democratizing design.

**Prompt keywords**: `modernist design, functionalist design, international style, clean geometric, rational design, le corbusier inspired, modern movement, less is more, mid-century modernism`

## 7. Minimalism (1960s-Present)

**Core principle**: Extreme reduction to essential elements only. "Less is more" taken to its logical conclusion. Deliberate simplicity as aesthetic and philosophical choice. Every single element must justify its existence.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Maximum negative space dominating compositions
- Only absolutely essential elements remain—everything else eliminated
- Clean lines and geometric forms (often just rectangles)
- Zero decoration or ornamentation of any kind
- Monochromatic or severely limited color palette
- Deliberate, intentional simplicity requiring great discipline
- Single focal points in vast empty space
- Precise, careful placement of few elements
- Silence and pause as positive design qualities

**Color palette**: Extremely limited, often monochromatic. White space as dominant "color." Single accent color if any. Neutral tones (black, white, gray, beige). Occasional bold single color for maximum impact against emptiness.

**Typography**: Clean sans-serifs with no decorative qualities—Helvetica, Futura, Proxima Nova, Univers. Generous letter-spacing and line-height. Minimal text quantity. Single typeface per project. Light to regular weights preferred. Type as quiet, not loud.

**Key elements**: Simple geometric shapes (rectangles, circles, lines), vast emptiness treated as active element, single focal points drawing all attention, deliberate absence and removal, breathing room around everything, perfect alignment and spacing.

**Feeling**: Calm, sophisticated, elegant, intentional, quiet, contemplative, pure, focused, serene, meditative, luxurious through restraint.

**Key figures**: Donald Judd, Agnes Martin, Robert Morris (art); Dieter Rams, Jony Ive (product design); John Pawson (architecture); Kenya Hara, Naoto Fukasawa (Japanese design).

**Philosophies**: "Less is more" (Mies van der Rohe); "Good design is as little design as possible" (Dieter Rams); "Emptiness is not nothingness" (Kenya Hara); "Perfection is achieved not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away" (Antoine de Saint-Exupéry).

**Prompt keywords**: `minimalist design, negative space design, essential only, maximum reduction, less is more, white space dominant, dieter rams style, japanese minimalism, clean simple design`

## 8. Brutalism (1950s-1970s Architecture, 2010s Digital Revival)

**Core principle**: Raw, unfinished aesthetic exposing structure and materials honestly. "Béton brut" means raw concrete in French. Anti-prettiness as democratic honesty. Monumental forms for social institutions.

**Visual characteristics (architecture)**:
- Raw exposed concrete left unfinished, showing formwork marks
- Massive, monumental scale projecting institutional presence
- Geometric, fortress-like forms with dramatic shadows
- Exposed structural elements—nothing hidden behind facades
- Repetitive modular units creating rhythm
- Rough textures celebrated rather than smoothed away
- Heavy, earth-bound presence anchoring to ground
- Dramatic cantilevers and overhangs

**Visual characteristics (digital/graphic)**:
- Raw HTML/default styling appearance celebrated
- Exposed structure (visible grids, system elements, functional components)
- High contrast, stark black and white layouts
- Intentionally "ugly" or harsh against polished design trends
- Monospace fonts and system typefaces as aesthetic choice
- Anti-design as deliberate design statement
- Dense information presentation without decoration

**Color palette**: Concrete grays in all variations. Raw material colors (rust, wood, steel). Digital: stark monochrome, harsh/jarring color combinations when used, pure black and white, sometimes brutal neon accents.

**Typography**: System fonts, monospace (Courier, Monaco), default HTML styling, bold harsh sans-serifs, exposed code-like text, no typographic refinement or polish, Times New Roman used ironically.

**Key elements**: Visible grid structures, raw borders without softening, stark high-contrast imagery, intentional roughness, honest material expression, no decorative polish, exposed mechanical systems, harsh lighting.

**Feeling**: Honest, confrontational, intellectual, anti-decorative, powerful, democratic, raw, challenging, institutional, serious, uncompromising.

**Digital examples**: brutalistwebsites.com collection; Yale School of Art website; Bloomberg Businessweek digital design.

**Prompt keywords**: `brutalist design, raw concrete, exposed structure, digital brutalism, harsh contrast, honest materials, béton brut, monumental brutalist, concrete architecture`

## 15. Post-Modernism (1970s-1990s)

**Core principle**: Rejection of Modernist rules through irony, eclecticism, historical quotation, and playful subversion. "Less is a bore" (Venturi). Complexity and contradiction welcomed. Double-coding allows multiple simultaneous meanings.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Historical quotation and pastiche mixing periods freely
- Ironic juxtaposition of high culture and low/pop culture
- Deliberate rule-breaking for expressive effect
- Ornamentation revival (often used with knowing irony)
- Eclectic combinations of disparate styles and periods
- Double-coding allowing multiple readings by different audiences
- Humor, wit, and playfulness in serious design
- Complexity over simplicity, contradiction over purity
- Surface pattern and decoration celebrated
- Vernacular and commercial imagery elevated

**Color palette**: Pastel pinks, mauves, teals, mint, salmon, lavender. Also bold clashing color combinations. Unexpected pairings that modernists would reject. Gradients common. Pattern and texture mixing.

**Typography**: Mixing historical typefaces with modern ones. Decorative revivals from various eras. Layered type creating visual complexity. Deliberately "wrong" combinations. Multiple typefaces clashing intentionally. Type with quotation marks (literal or implied).

**Key elements**: Classical architectural elements used ironically (columns, pediments, arches), geometric patterns from various eras combined, historical motifs in anachronistic contexts, layered collage compositions, visible "quotation marks" around borrowed elements.

**Feeling**: Ironic, playful, intellectual, eclectic, referential, subversive, questioning, humorous, knowing, self-aware, complex, inclusive.

**Key figures**: Robert Venturi & Denise Scott Brown (architecture/theory), Michael Graves, Memphis Group (Ettore Sottsass), Wolfgang Weingart, April Greiman, David Carson, Tibor Kalman.

**Era context**: Reaction against Modernist "less is more" dogma; embrace of complexity, contradiction, and historical reference; "Learning from Las Vegas" (1972); 1980s culture of excess and quotation.

**Prompt keywords**: `postmodern design, 1980s eclectic, ironic design, pastel geometric, historical pastiche, robert venturi, memphis influenced, complexity and contradiction, postmodern architecture`

## 20. Pop Art (1950s-1970s)

**Core principle**: Elevating mass culture, consumer products, and commercial imagery to fine art status. Blurring and questioning the boundary between "high art" and popular/commercial culture. Art for everyone, from everywhere.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Bold, flat colors with hard edges (no subtle gradation)
- Ben-Day dots replicating commercial printing's halftone patterns
- Commercial and advertising imagery appropriated directly
- Repetition and seriality (same image repeated with variations)
- High contrast with strong graphic impact
- Comic book and advertising aesthetics as fine art
- Bold black outlines defining shapes clearly
- Mundane consumer objects elevated and enlarged

**Color palette**: Primary colors (red, yellow, blue) used boldly. Black outlines defining shapes. CMYK printing colors visible. Limited palette per piece for graphic impact. Bright, commercial, "cheap" color sensibility.

**Typography**: Comic book lettering with bold outlines. Advertising display type from commercial sources. Bold sans-serifs from packaging. Speech bubbles and thought balloons. Headlines and slogans as art.

**Key elements**: Consumer products (soup cans, Coke bottles, soap boxes), celebrity portraits (Marilyn, Elvis, Mao), comic strip panels, advertising imagery appropriated wholesale, repeated motifs in grids, visible halftone/Ben-Day dots, brand logos and packaging.

**Feeling**: Ironic yet celebratory, bold, accessible, democratic, mass-culture commentary, satirical, vibrant, detached, cool, questioning value hierarchies.

**Key figures**: Andy Warhol, Roy Lichtenstein, Jasper Johns, James Rosenquist, Richard Hamilton (UK), Claes Oldenburg, Tom Wesselmann, Robert Indiana.

**Era context**: Post-WWII consumer boom, mass media explosion (television), reaction against Abstract Expressionism's emotional intensity, celebrity culture emergence, advertising industry's golden age, questioning what "art" means.

**Prompt keywords**: `pop art style, andy warhol style, roy lichtenstein, ben-day dots, comic book art, bold primary colors, mass culture imagery, campbell soup aesthetic, pop art portrait`

## 41. Memphis Design (1981-1988)

**Core principle**: Postmodern rejection of "good taste" minimalism through clashing patterns, bold colors, and playful irreverence. Anti-functionalism—form defies function. Design as joyful expression, not rational problem-solving.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Clashing bold patterns layered together (terrazzo, squiggles, geometric)
- Asymmetric, unexpected, "wrong" compositions
- Mix of geometric shapes and organic, cartoon-like forms
- Cheap materials (laminate, plastic) celebrated not hidden
- Playful, rule-breaking arrangements defying design logic
- Surface decoration loudly celebrated over form
- Cartoon-like, childish quality embraced
- Deliberate kitsch and "bad taste" as rebellion

**Color palette**: Bold, intentionally clashing combinations—hot pink, electric blue, acid yellow, turquoise, mint green, coral, black. Unexpected combinations that create tension. Pastels mixed with brights. Patterns combining multiple colors chaotically.

**Typography**: Multiple styles mixed freely without logic. Playful, irregular letterforms. Breaking every typographic convention. Pattern-filled letters. Cartoon-like, hand-drawn quality. Bold display faces at odd angles.

**Key elements**: Terrazzo speckle patterns, squiggle/wiggle lines, triangles and circles as decorations, bold zigzag stripes, unexpected color pairings, confetti-like scattered patterns, laminate textures, geometric totems.

**Feeling**: Playful, irreverent, chaotic, joyful, anti-establishment, energetic, rebellious, fun, ironic, deliberately "wrong," celebratory of bad taste.

**Key figures**: Ettore Sottsass (founder), Michele De Lucchi, Nathalie Du Pasquier, George Sowden, Martine Bedin, Peter Shire, Memphis Group collective (Milan, Italy).

**Era context**: 1980s rebellion against modernist "good taste" and minimalism; influence on MTV graphics, fashion, interior design; named after Bob Dylan song "Stuck Inside of Mobile with the Memphis Blues Again"; short-lived but hugely influential.

**Prompt keywords**: `memphis design, 1980s postmodern, terrazzo pattern, clashing colors, squiggle pattern, ettore sottsass, confetti geometric, bold 80s memphis, memphis milano`

## 49. Naive Art / Primitive Art (Various Periods)

**Core principle**: Untrained aesthetic with childlike simplicity, directness, and folk art authenticity. Art made without formal academic training. Direct emotional expression valued over technical virtuosity.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Simplified forms reduced to essential recognizable shapes
- Flat perspective ignoring academic rules of depth
- Bright, unblended colors applied intuitively
- Disproportionate scale based on importance or emotion, not reality
- "Incorrect" perspective and anatomy by academic standards
- Direct, unfiltered personal expression
- Handmade quality showing the maker's touch
- Visible brushstrokes and imperfect edges
- Narrative scenes telling stories simply

**Color palette**: Bright, saturated, often primary colors. Unmodulated solid color fills without shading. Intuitive, unexpected combinations. Colors often symbolic rather than realistic (blue tree, red sky).

**Typography**: Hand-drawn, irregular letterforms full of personality. Letters integrated naturally with imagery. Unpracticed but charming quality. Folk lettering traditions, sign-painting influences.

**Key elements**: Simplified human and animal figures, flat backgrounds without atmospheric perspective, symbolic objects larger than surroundings, folk motifs and decorative patterns, narrative scenes of daily life or memory, pattern fills decorating forms.

**Feeling**: Innocent, authentic, joyful, direct, personal, unselfconscious, charming, sincere, heartfelt, nostalgic, universal.

**Key figures**: Henri Rousseau ("Le Douanier"), Grandma Moses (Anna Mary Robertson Moses), Séraphine Louis, Alfred Wallis, Horace Pippin, Bill Traylor.

**Modern applications**: Children's book illustration, folk-inspired branding, craft and handmade product packaging, indie video game art, "outsider" aesthetic in contemporary design.

**Prompt keywords**: `naive art style, folk art illustration, childlike simplicity, henri rousseau style, primitive painting, outsider art, hand-drawn simple, untrained aesthetic, folk art design`
